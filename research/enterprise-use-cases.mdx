# Enterprise agentic use-case research

**Retrieval date:** July 13, 2026. Product capabilities and market adoption are time-sensitive; revalidate sources before a major release.

> **Status: Research.** Facts describe public documentation. Inferences synthesize reusable architecture implications. Recommendations select examples for ARA; they are not vendor claims or market rankings.

## Selection method

A scenario qualifies as a full ARA worked example when it satisfies most of these criteria:

```text
material enterprise demand
a multi-step outcome rather than text generation alone
one or more governed effects
state or evidence that survives a request
meaningful failure and recovery behavior
security, privacy, approval, or tenant boundaries
measurable outcome beyond subjective prose quality
portability across providers and orchestration products
```

ARA deliberately avoids examples that are merely chat wrappers, one-shot summarizers, or product-specific demos.

## Prioritized full examples

### 1. Software change delivery

High demand because engineering organizations already delegate repository research, bug fixes, tests, documentation, and pull-request creation to coding agents.

Architecture coverage:

```text
repository and issue context
sandboxed code/tool execution
long-running background session
branch and artifact lineage
CI and security verification
human review and merge authority
cost and model-route governance
```

### 2. Customer service case resolution

High demand because service organizations handle large volumes of repetitive but policy-sensitive cases across chat, email, CRM, billing, orders, identity, and knowledge systems.

Architecture coverage:

```text
conversation versus authoritative case state
customer identity and consent
retrieval provenance
read-only and mutating tools
SLA timers and escalation
PII and regional policy
human handoff with complete context
quality, containment, and customer-outcome evaluation
```

### 3. IT incident response and controlled remediation

High demand because incident work already requires event ingestion, evidence gathering, diagnosis, communication, change controls, rollback, and post-incident learning.

Architecture coverage:

```text
correlated events and durable waits
parallel diagnostic branches
service ownership and blast radius
read-only diagnostics versus production mutation
change approval and maintenance windows
ambiguous tool outcomes and rollback
SLO, audit, and post-incident evidence
```

### 4. Procure-to-pay exception resolution

High demand because invoices, purchase orders, receipts, contracts, supplier records, tax data, and payment controls regularly disagree and require expensive manual review.

Architecture coverage:

```text
document extraction and provenance
three-way match and deterministic arithmetic
supplier and fraud policy
segregation of duties
ERP mutation with idempotency/reconciliation
regional and retention requirements
financial audit and cost attribution
```

### 5. Synthetic research experiment

This is not a replacement for human research, but it is architecturally valuable because it exercises experiment variants, independent trials, persistent participant identities, deliberation iterations, scheduling fairness, and model sensitivity.

## Additional enterprise candidates

These are good pattern or future-example candidates, but do not all need full worked examples in ARA 1.0:

| Candidate | Why useful | Why not currently a full example |
|---|---|---|
| Security-alert triage and remediation | High-impact tools, adversarial inputs, containment, incident evidence | Overlaps the incident-response execution shape; add when security-specific contracts need deeper treatment |
| Employee onboarding and access fulfillment | Identity, HR, IT, approval, least privilege, long waits | Customer service and procurement already cover request/approval patterns |
| Contract review and obligation tracking | Document evidence, legal review, deadlines, negotiation | Requires jurisdiction- and legal-practice-specific caveats |
| Insurance claims | Documents, fraud, external parties, payments, human review | Industry rules vary substantially; useful for a regulated extension |
| KYC/AML investigation | Entity resolution, provenance, sanctions, human decision, audit | High-risk domain that should follow independent legal and domain review |
| Sales proposal/RFP response | Retrieval, drafting, pricing, approvals, source attribution | Often becomes a document-generation example unless downstream actions are included |
| Supply-chain exception management | Events, inventory, logistics, suppliers, re-planning | Strong future multi-party/A2A scenario |
| Healthcare administrative coordination | Scheduling, authorization, records, communications | Avoid clinical-decision implications without specialist review |

## Example-design rules

1. **Do not claim autonomy as the goal.** Use the least powerful control model that achieves the business outcome.
2. **Keep system-of-record authority outside the model.** Model outputs become proposals or typed evidence.
3. **Show at least one injected failure.** A happy path does not teach durable architecture.
4. **Show human authority precisely.** “Human in the loop” is insufficient without action binding, timing, role, and escalation.
5. **Measure the real outcome.** Resolution, successful change, restored service, correct accounting, or valid experiment evidence matters more than fluent text.
6. **Disclose limitations.** Examples state what remains product-, domain-, provider-, and jurisdiction-specific.

## Portfolio decision

ARA 1.0 keeps five complete examples. New examples should be added only when they introduce a materially different trust boundary, execution shape, or conformance requirement. Otherwise extend the pattern catalog or one existing worked example.
